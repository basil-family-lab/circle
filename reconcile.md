# Reconcile the Intention

*A Pattern for Adaptive Change Leadership by* **Michael Basil**

*For facilitators, team leads, or change agents when clarity exists but alignment and coordinated action are missing.*

## Summary

Take a group through **iterative, structured conversations** anchored on a **visible alignment artifact**—a charter, principles document, or alignment canvas—that captures collective intent. The primary value is **not in producing the artifact**, but in the **conversations themselves**, which surface assumptions, reconcile ambiguity, and create shared understanding.  

The artifact becomes a **living reference**, guiding internal coordination, cross-team collaboration, management decisions, and even downstream systems like AI context engineering. Over time, these conversations strengthen culture, decision-making, and alignment across the organization.

## Story

A department had been operating for months with active participation, yet actions were inconsistent. Teams assumed alignment, but priorities conflicted, responsibilities overlapped, and key social leaders remained unengaged.  

A facilitator introduced a **reconciliation exercise** using a visible artifact—a stale charter that no longer reflected the current culture. Through several iterative sessions, the group surfaced hidden assumptions, clarified principles, and aligned on priorities.  

Key stakeholders—vocal and quiet—reviewed and confirmed the emerging alignment. The artifact evolved alongside the conversation, **reflecting both intent and culture**.  

Over time, the group developed shared language, improved cross-team negotiation, and created a coherent foundation for action. Decisions became more strategic, conflict was reduced, and alignment extended beyond the team itself.  

It all began with one move:

**Reconcile the Intention.**

## Context

You are guiding a group that:

- has emerging clarity but **incomplete or inconsistent mental models**  
- lacks a coherent North Star or aligned principles  
- experiences tension or conflict due to ambiguity  
- may have stale or misaligned artifacts (charters, principles, or norms)  
- needs alignment for cross-team coordination or broader organizational influence  

Traditional methods—emails, meetings, or documentation—raise awareness but rarely create **true alignment or shared understanding**. Without deliberate intervention, ambiguity persists and actions remain fragmented.

## Problem

How do you **connect clarity to coordinated action** when:

- key mental models are fragmented or incomplete  
- some voices are unengaged or hidden  
- the group lacks shared language and priorities  
- existing artifacts are misaligned with culture or outdated  
- organizational influence extends beyond the immediate team  

You need a **conversation-centered exercise** that creates alignment without relying solely on formal outputs.

## Forces

### Clarity vs. Ambiguity

The group may understand parts of the system, but collective understanding is incomplete or inconsistent.

### Alignment vs. Autonomy

Individuals act according to personal understanding; alignment requires surfaced assumptions and explicit reconciliation.

### Iteration vs. Completion

Agreement in one meeting is often superficial. Key voices must lean in multiple times to confirm alignment.

### Visibility vs. Safety

Publishing the artifact encourages engagement and accountability but invites scrutiny and risk.

### Culture vs. Codification

Artifacts must reflect **actual practices and priorities**, not just formal documentation.

## Solution

**Reconcile the Intention.**

- Anchor the conversation on a **visible alignment artifact**. The artifact is the reference point, **not the goal**.  
- Lead **iterative, structured conversations** to surface assumptions, reconcile tensions, and clarify principles, priorities, and relationships.  
- Engage **key social leaders** multiple times to ensure alignment and uncover hidden ambiguity.  
- Begin with **principles**, then define priorities, responsibilities, and relationships.  
- Make the artifact **visible** to internal teams, collaborators, management, or downstream systems like AI.  
- Treat the conversation as a **cultural exercise** that strengthens trust, shared understanding, and alignment.  
- Revisit periodically to maintain **coherence, cultural fidelity, and evolving understanding**.  

## Resulting Context

When this pattern works:

- Shared language and principles emerge  
- Mental models become **coherent and actionable**  
- Actions and decisions are **aligned with collective intent**  
- Artifact serves as a **living reference** for engagement, coordination, and AI-informed processes  
- Organizational culture strengthens, improving collaboration, negotiation, and decision-making  

Over time, alignment ripples outward, creating coherence across teams and departments. Yet the essential move remains simple:

**Reconcile the Intention.**

## Key Characteristics

- Conversation-centered: artifact anchors the exercise, but value is in dialogue  
- Iterative: engages key voices multiple times  
- Bridges clarity to action  
- Visible artifact supports internal and external audiences  
- Strengthens culture, alignment, and decision-making  
- Works as a recurring practice, not a one-off exercise  

## Failure Modes

### One-and-Done Alignment

*Description*: Agreement is assumed after a single meeting; key voices remain unengaged.  
*Mitigation*: Iterate, revisit, and confirm with all relevant participants.

### Surface-Level Agreement

*Description*: Superficial consensus masks hidden tensions or assumptions.  
*Mitigation*: Facilitate structured conversations, probe ambiguity, and challenge assumptions.

### Artifact Disconnected from Reality

*Description*: Artifact reflects formal intent but not actual culture or priorities.  
*Mitigation*: Align conversation with lived experience and cultural context.

### Lack of Visibility / Engagement

*Description*: Artifact is inaccessible or ignored by intended audiences.  
*Mitigation*: Publish thoughtfully, invite feedback, and use as reference for downstream processes.

### Overly Complex Artifact

*Description*: Attempting to codify everything creates clutter and confusion.  
*Mitigation*: Focus on essentials: principles, priorities, key relationships.
